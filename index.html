01 — THE CONCEPT

What Are Contour Lines?

Imagine slicing a mountain horizontally — like cutting a loaf of bread into even layers. Each slice represents a specific elevation. Now look at those slices from directly above. That's your contour map.

Every contour line connects points of equal elevation. If you walked along a single contour line in real life, you'd stay perfectly level — no uphill, no downhill. The magic is in what happens between the lines.

HOW 3D BECOMES 2D — SIDE VIEW vs TOP-DOWN

Three Things Every Contour Line Tells You

1. Elevation — Each line sits at a specific height above sea level. Index lines (the thicker, bolder ones) are labeled with their elevation.

2. Shape of the terrain — The curves and bends of contour lines mirror the shape of the land. Circular lines? A hill. Long parallel lines? A ridge.

3. Steepness — This is the biggest unlock. Lines close together = steep terrain. Lines far apart = gentle slope. It's that simple, and once you see it, you can't unsee it.

02 — LINE TYPES

Know Your Lines

Not all contour lines are created equal. There are three types, and knowing them is essential.

THE THREE LINE TYPES

Index Lines — The thick, bold lines labeled with elevation numbers. Usually every 5th contour line. These are your anchors.

Intermediate Lines — The thinner, unlabeled lines between index lines. Count these to figure out specific elevations.

Supplementary Lines — Dashed lines used in areas with very gentle terrain where standard intervals would miss important features.

CONTOUR INTERVAL → The elevation difference between adjacent contour lines. Always check your map legend first — a 40-foot interval tells a very different story than a 10-foot interval.

04 — CORE RULES

The Rules of Contour Lines

These rules are absolute. They never break. Memorize them and you have the foundation for reading any topo map on the planet.

Closed Loops = Hilltop

Concentric closed contour lines indicate a summit or hilltop. The innermost ring is the highest point.

Tick Marks = Depression

Closed loops with small tick marks pointing inward mean the ground dips down — a crater, sinkhole, or depression.

V's Point Uphill = Valley

When contour lines form a V-shape, the V points toward higher elevation. That's a valley, draw, or drainage.

V's Point Down = Ridge

V-shapes pointing toward lower elevation indicate a ridge or spur — high ground extending outward.

Lines Never Cross

Contour lines never intersect. One point can't be two different elevations. If lines appear to touch, it's a cliff.

Lines Always Close

Every contour line forms a complete loop — even if it extends beyond the edge of your map.

05 — TERRAIN FEATURES

Terrain Feature Glossary

Tap any card to expand the full field description of each terrain feature.

Saddle (Col)

Low point between two higher peaks — looks like an hourglass pinch.

The low point on a ridge crest between two peaks. On a topo map, look for the hourglass shape where two sets of concentric circles nearly touch. Saddles are natural crossing points and often where trails cross ridges. In the military, these are called "cols" and are critical terrain features for route planning.

Ridge

Elongated high ground. Contour V's point away from the ridge toward lower ground.

A long, narrow area of high ground. Contour lines form U or V shapes pointing DOWNHILL (toward lower elevation). Ridges make great navigation handrails — follow the high ground between the contour bends. When hiking, ridges often offer the driest footing and best visibility.

Draw (Valley)

The opposite of a ridge — a low channel. V's point uphill. Water collects and flows here.

The low ground between two ridges or spurs. Contour V-shapes point UPHILL toward higher elevation. Water collects here — streams and rivers run through draws. In the field, draws are natural paths for water drainage. If you're looking for water in backcountry, follow the draws.

Spur

A finger of high ground jutting out from a ridge. U-shape points downhill.

A finger of higher ground extending out from a ridge. Similar to a ridge but shorter and jutting to the side. Contour lines form U-shapes pointing downhill. Spurs are excellent landmarks for navigation — they're distinct enough to identify from a distance and confirm your position on the map.

Cliff

Lines stacking extremely close or merging. Dangerously steep — possibly vertical.

When contour lines stack extremely close together or appear to merge, you're looking at near-vertical terrain. Some maps use special cliff symbols (tiny tick marks). If planning a route, cliffs are impassable — navigate around them. On USGS maps, a cliff band often shows as a thick brown smear.

Flat / Gentle Slope

Widely spaced lines mean very gradual elevation change.

Very widely spaced contour lines indicate minimal elevation change. In extremely flat areas, you may see dashed supplementary lines at half the normal contour interval to show subtle features that would otherwise be invisible. Flat areas near water often indicate marshland or flood plains.
06 — FIELD CRAFT

Pro Tips

Beyond the basics — these are the things that separate a topo-reader from a topo-master.

Reading Water Flow

Water always flows perpendicular to contour lines, from high elevation to low. Find the V-shaped contour lines pointing uphill — that's your drainage. A stream will run right down the center of that V. The tighter the V, the steeper and faster the water.

QUICK TEST → Drop an imaginary marble on the map. It rolls perpendicular to the contour lines toward lower elevation. Where marbles from different directions converge? That's where you'll find water.

Estimating Trail Steepness

Count how many contour lines your trail crosses in a given distance. If your map has a 40-foot contour interval and your trail crosses 5 lines in half a mile, that's 200 feet of elevation gain — a steady but manageable climb. Cross 15 lines in the same distance? That's 600 feet. Prepare your legs.

RULE OF THUMB → On a USGS 7.5-minute quad (1:24,000 scale), one inch on the map ≈ 2,000 feet on the ground. Use this with contour counting to estimate grade.

Navigating with Contour Lines

When the trail disappears or the fog rolls in, contour lines are your best friend. If you know your elevation (from an altimeter or GPS), you know you're somewhere on that contour line. Combine that with a single identifiable terrain feature — a ridge, a creek, a saddle — and you've got a fix on your position.

Reading Aspect

Aspect is which direction a slope faces. This matters more than most people realize. South-facing slopes get more sun — less snow in winter, drier in summer. North-facing slopes hold snow longer, stay cooler, have different vegetation.

HUNTER'S TIP → Game tends to feed on south-facing slopes in morning (warming sun) and bed on north-facing slopes (shade and cover). Topo maps tell you this before you leave the truck.
